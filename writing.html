When Perfect Wasn't an Option

Navigating complexity under constraint

I was three weeks away from our first SAP focus group when the ground shifted beneath us.

I was serving as the Business Integration Lead for a company-wide SAP deployment at a 10,000-person professional services firm. After years of working with an outdated system, the Executive Committee had committed to a major transformation. My role was to design and facilitate a series of focus groups—showcasing new tools and processes to a pilot group of users across the organization. The goal was twofold: build buy-in for a change many employees were nervous about and gather feedback that would shape our testing, training, and deployment roadmap.

My initial approach carefully sequenced the sessions to mirror how people actually work—following the natural project and revenue cycle from client intake through time tracking and billing. But as our first focus group launch approached, we hit a problem: the first application in that sequence, where projects are created, wouldn't be ready in time. I had a week to figure out what to do, and none of the options were great.

We could delay all the sessions until the application was ready, but that risked a domino effect on downstream testing and training milestones we couldn't afford to miss. We could showcase the application using process wireframes instead of a live demo, but we'd already promised our pilot group they'd see the system in action—and first impressions matter, especially when people are already anxious about change. Or we could flip the script entirely: start with one of the applications that was ready—time tracking—even though it disrupted the logical flow we'd carefully designed.

I laid out all three options for our workstream leads and executive sponsor, walking through the trade-offs, risks, and potential mitigation strategies for each. There was debate. Some wanted to wait for the "right" sequence. Others worried about losing credibility if we changed course. But I pushed for option three. Momentum mattered more than perfection here, and I knew we could make it work.

"Great execution isn't about having all the pieces fall into place perfectly—it's about recognizing when the plan needs to change and solving for the outcome, not the original process."

The solution wasn't just switching the order—it was redesigning the experience. I expanded the opening content to anchor participants in the overall revenue cycle process, so they understood the bigger picture even if we were starting mid-cycle. I built in review content for the later sessions to refresh everyone's memory and create continuity. What could have felt chaotic instead felt intentional.

The focus groups succeeded. Feedback survey responses were overwhelmingly positive, and our executive sponsor was pleased we wrapped ahead of the first testing cycle. More importantly, we protected the project timeline and maintained trust with a group of employees who were already skeptical about such a massive change.

What I learned: great execution isn't about having all the pieces fall into place perfectly. It's about recognizing when the plan needs to change, making a clear-eyed decision about trade-offs, and then solving for the outcome—not the original process.

Two Truths at Once

Translating between strategy and execution

The CFO was worried the university was about to default on its debt coverage ratio. I was tasked with building a five-year financial forecasting model that would give the Board a clear picture of where things stood—and more importantly, what needed to change.

I spent weeks meeting with department heads and P&L owners across the institution, digging into their activity drivers and building their assumptions into the model. It wasn't simple. Most of these leaders were used to thinking one year out for annual budgeting, not five. But eventually, we got there. The model produced a comprehensive financial forecast with input from every key stakeholder across the university.

Before presenting to the Board, the CFO asked us to run the numbers by the Treasurer—a seasoned veteran who knew the institution's cash position better than anyone. When he reviewed the forecast, he didn't like what he saw. Not because it was wrong, but because it showed too much cushion. The department heads' projections suggested a longer runway than he believed actually existed. He had information they didn't: upcoming bond payment schedules, large capital outlays on the horizon, cash balance realities that painted a grimmer picture.

We had a choice to make. We could stick with the forecast built on stakeholder input, or we could let the Treasurer's concerns override the work we'd done. Neither felt right.

Instead, we created space for both perspectives. We embedded the Treasurer's assumptions into the model's worst-case scenario and gave it its own section in the Board presentation—clearly labeled and separate from the base forecast. When we presented, we explained that we didn't think the worst case was the most likely outcome, but it was a genuine possibility if more aggressive cost-cutting measures weren't implemented soon.

"Advisory work isn't about choosing which stakeholder to believe—it's about creating frameworks that can hold multiple perspectives without collapsing into confusion."

The Board needed to see both. The department forecast showed what was possible if operations continued as planned. The Treasurer's scenario showed what could happen if cash realities caught up faster than expected. Two truths at once.

The political reality? The CFO left not long after, and the organization kicked the can down the road. But the worst-case scenario we presented wasn't theoretical—it was a warning based on real data. Without that dual framing, the Board would have kept delaying hard decisions until they genuinely faced a debt default.

What I learned: advisory work isn't about choosing which stakeholder to believe. It's about creating frameworks that can hold multiple perspectives without collapsing into confusion. Two things can be true at the same time, as long as you clearly communicate the drivers behind each scenario. The best analysis doesn't pick sides—it gives decision-makers the full picture and trusts them to act on it, even when the politics make that hard.

All Of Me

A reflection on self-discovery

I see you, Strength, when life is hard and difficult to surmount, I do feel you, Love, drowning in uncharted waters, no matter the amount, I'm challenged by you, Anger, and your unwelcome entry into my soul, I want to know you, Calm, I believe it may be you who can make me feel more whole. I wish I lived in your house, Forgiveness, the things you could teach, I've never needed you, Pride, your tight grip makes me hard to reach, I am all of these mangled pieces orchestrated into one, Do they recognize me in all their madness? By me are they equally undone?
